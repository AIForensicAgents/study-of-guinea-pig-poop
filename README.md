# study-of-guinea-pig-poop

A compact, professional — and slightly cheeky — repository documenting a field- and lab-friendly study of guinea pig scat for educational and biological purposes.

---

## Overview

This repository collects observations, photographic examples, and practical notes about guinea pig feces (scat, droppings) with the goal of supporting classroom biology, citizen science, and basic health diagnostics. The work focuses on non-invasive observation, morphology description, and simple inference about diet and habitat from droppings. This is not veterinary advice; it is an observational guide intended to augment learning and responsible field practice.

- Scope: morphology, photographic examples, contextual clues (diet/habitat), safe handling guidance, reproducible note-taking.
- Target audience: educators, students, naturalists, field technicians, and curious pet owners.
- Tone: professional, evidence-minded, and approachable.

---

## Why it matters

Guinea pig feces can be surprisingly informative. Observing droppings can:

- Provide quick, non-invasive clues about **diet and digestive health**.
- Support **education** in zoology, digestion, and animal ecology using a common, accessible model organism.
- Offer data points for **behavioral** and **habitat** inference when combined with other observations (e.g., feeding sites, bedding composition).
- Serve as a gateway for introducing students to **sampling protocols**, photographic metadata, and ethical fieldwork.

Small, frequent evidence like droppings can reveal trends that individual observational snapshots miss. Collecting structured notes improves reproducibility and teaches scientific habits.

## Morphology notes

Observe and record the following features for each sample:

- Shape and size
  - Guinea pig droppings are typically small, sausage- or pellet-shaped. Note length (mm) and approximate diameter.
- Color
  - Fresh droppings vary from green (more fresh vegetation) to brown (more dried or mixed diets). Note hue and any gradients.
- Consistency and surface texture
  - Firm pellets vs. soft/crumbly stool may indicate recent dietary shifts, hydration, or digestive upset.
- Contents and inclusions
  - Visible fiber, undigested seed hulls, or plant fragments give diet clues.
- Odor (qualitatively)
  - Strong changes in odor can indicate spoilage or infection; record only descriptive notes (e.g., “sharp/sour”).
- Arrangement and quantity
  - Groupings, latrine sites, and frequency inform social/territorial behavior and population density when observed outdoors.

Record metadata with each sample:
- Date/time, GPS (if outdoors), local weather, observed nearby food sources, animal IDs (if captive), and photo/exif data.

Example quick template (one-line):
`2026-03-21 | 09:15 | Cage A | pellet, 7–9 mm x 5 mm | dark brown, firm | visible hay fibers | photo: img001.jpg`

## Habitat / Diet clues

Droppings often encode what an animal has eaten recently. Interpret with caution and corroborate with direct observation.

Dietary clues:
- Green, leafy-rich droppings: recent consumption of fresh greens or high-moisture plants.
- Brown, compact pellets: high-fiber hay-based diets or drier food.
- Presence of seeds/pellet crumbs: consumption of commercial mixes.
- Oily or greasy coatings: high-fat treats or seeds; note infrequency.

Habitat and behavior clues:
- Location clustering (latrines) suggests territory marking or preferred latrine behavior.
- Mixed substrates (bedding, soil, seed hulls) indicate the proximate feeding environment.
- Freshness + proximity to shelters or feeding stations can reveal foraging patterns.

Limitations:
- Single droppings are ambiguous — combine multiple lines of evidence.
- Environmental degradation (rain/sun) quickly alters color/consistency outdoors.

## Ethics / Safety

Safety and ethics are paramount. Follow these guidelines:

- Animal welfare
  - Never stress or handle animals solely to obtain samples. Observe first; collect only from surfaces or bedding where animals are not currently located.
  - For captive studies, obtain owner/manager permission and follow institutional animal care guidelines.
- Personal safety & hygiene
  - Wear disposable gloves when handling fecal matter.
  - Use a face covering if aerosolization is possible (e.g., dusty bedding).
  - Wash hands thoroughly with soap and water after handling.
  - Disinfect tools and surfaces with appropriate disinfectants (e.g., 70% ethanol or household bleach solutions as per label).
- Biosecurity
  - Treat all fecal samples as potentially infectious. Do not ingest or touch face during handling.
  - Avoid contact with immunocompromised individuals’ animals without veterinary oversight.
- Legal and ethical collections
  - Do not remove droppings from protected sites if prohibited.
  - Respect private property and obtain permissions for outdoor sampling.

This guide is educational only and not a replacement for professional veterinary or biosafety advice.
